Here’s a roundup of interesting independent/alternative geopolitics content I came across recently. These are the kinds of stories and angles that tend to fly under the radar of mainstream coverage.

---

## “Empty Streets, Shrinking Cities: China’s Economic Reality” — *Digging into China*

I stumbled on this [Digging into China video][1] and it really made me rethink what I thought I knew about China’s slowdown. The presenter walks through actual on-the-ground footage of empty malls, reduced mobility data, and ghost-city indicators, and connects them back to specific policy choices — housing tax experiments, local governments running out of land-sale revenue — rather than just writing it off as a cyclical dip. The argument is that China’s internal demand weakness is way more structural than mainstream pieces tend to acknowledge.

The thing that really caught my eye though: local treasury shortfalls are now cascading into reduced municipal services and infrastructure slowdowns, which then further depress household confidence. It is a self-reinforcing cycle. You see headlines about “economic data misses” all the time, but nobody really ties it back to how municipal financing actually works on the ground. That has real implications for how China’s fiscal responses play out and what it means for commodity demand going forward. There’s also a related deeper dive from the same channel [here][2].

---

## “Bizarre negotiations and intel back channels” — *The Duran* / Alex Christoforou

This [episode of The Duran][3] takes a closer look at the diplomatic back-channels around the Ukraine conflict and some of the recent US/EU signaling that has been happening. The hosts pull together leaked reporting, regional interviews, and diplomatic timelines to make the case that the negotiation dynamics right now are more fluid — and honestly less Washington-centric — than the routine coverage would have you believe. They also get into how non-state influencers and private envoys are actually shaping what options end up on the table, which I found fascinating.

One point that stuck with me: small diplomatic teams and private intermediaries — not the formal capitals — are increasingly the ones determining whether ceasefire concepts even survive early vetting. So when you see a headline about “failed talks,” it might actually be tactical repositioning rather than an outright collapse. That nuance gets lost in most mainstream summaries, and it changes how you read the situation quite a bit.

---

## Recent posts from *Geopolitics & Empire*

If you are into long-form interviews with regional experts, the [Geopolitics & Empire podcast and site][4] has been putting out some really solid content. Recent episodes cover the Middle East, Eurasia, and tech/AI geopolitics with people who actually know the ground reality. What I find valuable is how they tie ideological framing and technology controls back to concrete policy — like how surveillance tech and export controls end up as bargaining chips in diplomatic negotiations. The essays on the site are good at connecting cultural and ideological moves to real policy outcomes.

Something G&E keeps surfacing that I think deserves more attention: the non-traditional levers of diplomacy. Tech governance, sanctions design, private sector behavior — these are the things quietly reshaping geopolitical alignment. Mainstream outlets will cover sanctions or tech disputes as isolated events, but G&E digs into the long-tail institutional mechanisms like licensing regimes and data-localization rules that actually determine how alliances shift over time. It is a lens I have started finding really useful for thinking about longer-term strategy.
